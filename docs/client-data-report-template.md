# Ranking Atlas Data Report Landing Page

The spine of a Ranking Atlas data report. This document lays out the content layout only. No code. Use it when planning a new report with the editorial team, or when reviewing a draft before it goes into the template.

The page is built for two audiences in this order: journalists scanning for a citable figure, and operators reading top to bottom. Every section earns its place by serving one of those two reads.

---

## 1. Hero

The hero is the entire decision surface. A reader who only sees this block should still leave with the headline finding.

**Eyebrow label.** One of: Data Report, Guide, Essay. Data reports always carry "Data Report". Title Case. Centred.

**Headline (H1).** The report title. Start case, no full stop. Punchy but not clickbait. The title is the artefact's name and will be quoted by journalists in their first paragraph.

**Standfirst.** One sentence. The single sharpest finding, stated plainly with the headline figure inside it. No em dashes. Italic serif.

**Hero figure (optional).** A single illustrative image or chart. If a chart is used here, it earns the slot only when one chart can carry the entire argument visually. Otherwise omit the figure and let the standfirst breathe.

**Byline card.** Tinted card containing: "Content Written By:", author name, role, publication month and year. Data reports use "Ranking Atlas" institutionally per the authorship rules. Essays and guides use the founder's name.

**Navigation panel.** Tinted card with a "Navigation" heading and a hollow-bullet list of anchors. One anchor for Overview, one for Key Takeaways, one per finding section, one for the closing editorial. Anchor text matches the section H2.

---

## 2. Overview

Three paragraphs. No headings inside the section beyond the H2.

- Paragraph 1: state the headline pattern in the data and the figures that prove it. This paragraph carries the strongest claim in the report.
- Paragraph 2: describe what was measured, how, and against which baseline. Name the baseline source.
- Paragraph 3: state what the data shows and what it does not claim. This is the honesty clause that earns a journalist's trust.

---

## 3. Key Takeaways

A bulleted list, five to six items. Each bullet opens with a bold claim sentence containing the figure, followed by a supporting clause naming the source or the cohort.

These are the lines a journalist will copy verbatim. Write them that way.

---

## 4. Finding sections

One section per finding. Typical reports carry three to five.

Each finding section follows the same internal layout:

- H2 written as a claim, not a category label. "Kayako Raised Entry-Tier Prices by 427% in Six Years" beats "Largest Increases".
- Visual: chart or table. Chart preferred when the shape of the data is the point. Table preferred when the figures themselves are the point.
- Two to four paragraphs of prose. Open with the headline figure in plain English. Add context against the baseline. Close with the interpretive consequence.

Alternate section background between white and the tinted off-white so the page reads as distinct findings rather than one long column.

---

## 5. Closing editorial

The reframe. By this point the reader has the data. This section says what the data means.

- One H2 that reframes the central finding in interpretive terms.
- Two to three paragraphs of argument grounded in the figures above.
- A pull quote in the founder's voice, two or three sentences. The pull quote is the line that survives social sharing.
- Optional closing paragraph after the pull quote.

This is the only section in a data report where the voice is openly editorial.

---

## 6. Methodology

Disclosure section, structured as named sub-blocks so a journalist can verify the report in under two minutes.

Standard sub-blocks:

- What we asked. The queries, prompts, or measurements.
- Who we asked. The systems, sources, or respondents.
- Sample size. Total observations, unique entities, time window.
- Classification. How observations were categorised. Manual, automated, cascade.
- Exclusions. What was removed, why, and how many observations.
- Baselines. The reference figures the study measures against, with sources.

Write each block in two or three sentences. Do not pad.

---

## 7. Limitations

A short list of candid concessions about what the data does not prove. Four to six paragraphs, one limitation each.

Limitations are not a weakness in a Ranking Atlas report. They are the section that proves the rest of the report can be trusted.

---

## 8. Download the data

A single panel with the dataset filename, a one-sentence description of the columns, and a download button. The citation line sits below the panel: "Ranking Atlas, '[Report title],' [Month Year]."

If the dataset is not yet public, omit the section entirely rather than leave a placeholder.

---

## 9. Press contact sign-off

One short paragraph offering additional cuts of the data, methodology questions, or regional breakouts on request. The contact line points to the contact inbox.

Data reports do not carry hard sales CTAs. The sign-off is the only commercial surface on the page, and it is editorial in tone.

---

## 10. Related research

Three sibling resources, displayed as cards. Choose pieces that share an audience or a theme. Each card shows title, one-sentence description, and a link.

If the report stands alone in its topic area, link to the three nearest pieces in editorial voice rather than forcing topical adjacency.

---

## 11. Closing institutional bio

A short standardised block identifying Ranking Atlas as the publisher. Identical across all data reports.

---

## Voice and house rules

- British English throughout.
- No em dashes anywhere on the page. Use colons or sentence breaks.
- No hedging verbs: might, could, supports, suggests. State the finding.
- Active voice. Short sentences mixed with longer.
- Numbers are figures, not words, above ten.
- Do not name a target audience or ICP inside a data report. The study reads as primary-source research to any reader.
- Do not announce a link. Write the sentence with the linked phrase inline.

---

## What a finished page should feel like

A journalist should be able to land on the page, scan the headline, read the standfirst, copy a bullet from the Key Takeaways, verify the figure against the Methodology, and file a story without ever leaving the page.

An operator should be able to read the page top to bottom in fifteen minutes and walk away with one decision they did not have before.

If a section serves neither of those reads, cut it.
