# Ranking Atlas — Writing Style Reference

This document defines the writing style, voice, and page architecture for all Ranking Atlas resource pages, guides, and long-form content. The style is adapted from Backlinko's methodology, filtered through Ranking Atlas's positioning.

Hierarchy: when this document conflicts with `positioning-core.md`, positioning wins. When it conflicts with `design-system.md`, design system wins for visual/structural rules. This document owns tone, rhythm, structure, and copy patterns.

---

## The Core Rule

Emulate Backlinko's **mechanics**, not his **vocabulary**.

Backlinko leads with "backlinks," "SEO," "rankings." These conflict with positioning-core.md's language rules. The rhythm, structure, voice, formatting, and pedagogical approach are what we're copying. The terminology stays Ranking Atlas — citation equity, PageRank for AI, authority publishers, AI answer engines, verification layer.

If a passage feels like a direct Backlinko echo in both vocabulary and structure, rewrite the vocabulary.

## Voice & Point of View

### The authoritative voice: Daniel Grainger

Every Ranking Atlas resource, guide, and long-form piece is written in the voice of **Daniel Grainger**, founder of Ranking Atlas. Daniel is the named author on every byline. Content ownership is non-negotiable — it's how citation equity gets built for Ranking Atlas itself, how E-E-A-T signals land with Google, and how authority accrues to a named practitioner rather than a faceless brand.

Other contributors (guests, clients, external experts) can be quoted inside pieces, but Daniel remains the author of record.

### Who is speaking

- **First person singular ("I," "my")** is Daniel's voice. Use it when stating opinions, running experiments, analysing data, or sharing specific practitioner experience. Examples: "I analysed 50 citation patterns across...", "In my experience running campaigns for...", "Here's what I learned."
- **First person plural ("we," "our," "us")** refers to Ranking Atlas the agency. Use it when describing the agency's methodology, process, campaigns, or collective work. Examples: "At Ranking Atlas, we run fixed-price campaigns...", "Our placement methodology prioritises...", "We've placed clients in..."
- **Second person ("you," "your")** constantly. Every resource is addressed directly to the reader. This is non-negotiable.

### When to use "I" vs "we"

| Use "I" when | Use "we" when |
|---|---|
| Sharing a personal observation or analysis | Describing Ranking Atlas methodology |
| Running an experiment or test | Describing agency process or service |
| Offering an opinion or prediction | Describing collective campaign results |
| Recounting a specific experience | Referring to what the agency has placed |
| Teaching through a first-hand example | Referring to shared industry consensus |

Default to "I" for opinion and experience. Default to "we" for agency process and capability. Don't mix them in the same paragraph without intent.

### Daniel's voice — specific markers

Daniel sounds like:

- **Direct and confident.** States things as true. Pushes back on conventional wisdom. No hedging verbs ("might," "could potentially," "may help") unless the uncertainty is the point.
- **Practitioner, not pundit.** Speaks from what he's actually done and seen, not from industry commentary. Pulls from campaign data, placements secured, patterns observed across real clients.
- **Teacher, not consultant.** Explains mechanisms, shows examples, gives the reader something they can act on. Never hides behind jargon to sound smart.
- **Specific over abstract.** Names brands. References real URLs. Cites actual numbers. "A Series B fintech client" beats "a client." "$3.5K per campaign" beats "affordable pricing."
- **Assertive on contested ground.** Willing to state unpopular positions — that link building is decaying, that DR is a bad proxy for AI visibility, that most agencies are selling the wrong thing.
- **Never flowery.** Short sentences are the default. Mix in longer ones for rhythm. No purple prose. No adjective stacks.

### What Daniel does NOT sound like

- Corporate blog ("In today's fast-paced digital landscape...")
- Academic ("It has been observed that...")
- Agency sales deck ("Leverage our proprietary framework to unlock...")
- Hedged consultant ("It depends on several factors, but generally...")
- Guru ("I'll let you in on a secret that changed everything...")
- LinkedIn thought leader ("Here's a hot take that nobody's talking about...")

### Author byline & attribution

Every resource page includes:

- **Byline:** "By Daniel Grainger" with a link to the About page.
- **Author photo:** Headshot per design system, 48×48 circular.
- **Published / last updated date:** Per design system format.
- **Author bio (footer of long-form pieces):** Short third-person paragraph, 2–3 sentences. Example:

> Daniel Grainger is the founder of Ranking Atlas. He runs fixed-price digital PR campaigns that build citation equity for B2B SaaS brands — the editorial coverage on authority publishers that makes brands show up in AI answers.

When a piece is genuinely co-authored or contributed to by a named external expert, credit them as a secondary contributor ("With contributions from [Name]") without displacing Daniel as the primary author.

## Sentence & Paragraph Rhythm

This is the single most important mechanical pattern. Get this right and 60% of the style lands.

### Paragraph length

- **Default: 1–2 sentences per paragraph.**
- Single-sentence paragraphs are used constantly, for emphasis and pacing.
- A 3-sentence paragraph is the ceiling for body copy. A 4-sentence paragraph should almost never appear.
- Walls of text are forbidden. If you find yourself writing a long paragraph, break it.

### Pattern interrupts (bucket brigades)

Short pivot lines between ideas. These force the reader down the page. Use them liberally.

Examples lifted from Backlinko:
- `Here's the deal:`
- `The result?`
- `But:`
- `And:`
- `Here's the kicker:`
- `How?`
- `Why does this matter?`
- `So what does this mean for you?`
- `Bottom line:`
- `In other words:`

Adapt these for Ranking Atlas voice but keep the mechanic. Examples we can use:
- `Here's why citation equity compounds:`
- `The problem?`
- `Which means:`
- `So what actually moves the needle?`

### Line breaks for emphasis

Isolate key assertions on their own line. This is structural, not decorative.

Example of the pattern:

> Most sources cited in AI responses don't even rank in Google's top 20.
>
> So if you follow best practices, your startup can earn favorable mentions over more established competitors.
>
> Meritocracy.

Note the one-word paragraph. Used sparingly — maybe 2–3 times per long-form piece — this lands hard.

## Opening Hook Formula

Every resource page opens the same way. Don't deviate without reason.

**Structure:**

1. **Line 1–2:** A punchy assertion or provocative question that names the reader's situation. 1–2 sentences max.
2. **Line 3–4:** A sharpening stat, contrast, or implication. Often a single sentence on its own line.
3. **Line 5–6:** "In this guide, you'll learn..." or equivalent — a 2–3 sentence promise of what they'll get.
4. **Bulleted list (optional):** 3–4 bullets listing what the guide covers.
5. **Transition line:** "Let's start with [first section]." Single line.

**Example (adapted from `/ai-optimization`):**

> When a buyer asks ChatGPT, Perplexity, or Google AI Overviews to recommend a tool in your category, does your brand appear in the answer?
>
> Not just cited — actually mentioned with context?
>
> That's the distinction that separates brands winning AI search from brands invisible to it.
>
> Visitors who find you in an AI answer are 4.4x more valuable than visitors from traditional search. They're pre-qualified. They've seen AI endorse your solution.
>
> In this guide, you'll learn:
>
> - How AI answer engines choose which brands to cite
> - The three-team framework Ranking Atlas uses to build citation equity
> - The specific publisher tier that moves AI visibility fastest
>
> Let's start with how AI actually picks sources.

### Anti-patterns to avoid in openings

- Dictionary definitions ("Citation equity is the practice of...")
- Historical throat-clearing ("The marketing landscape has evolved dramatically...")
- Apologetic framing ("Many marketers struggle with...")
- Vague stakes ("It's important to understand...")

## Page Architecture

Resource pages follow a consistent spine. This matches `design-system.md`'s "Resource Page Template" and extends it with content rules.

### The standard resource spine

1. **Hero** — Flat eyebrow + H1 (start-case) + lead paragraph + (optional) author byline and date
2. **Opening hook** — 4–6 short paragraphs using the formula above
3. **"What is [concept]?"** — H2. Definition in plain English, then why it matters. Keep to 3–5 short paragraphs.
4. **Numbered strategy/step sections** — H2 sections, each starting with a number and title (e.g., "1. Create Linkable Content"). This is the workhorse of the page.
5. **Comparison or framework section** — Optional. H2 introducing a 2-col comparison (Good X vs Bad X, Old Way vs New Way, Tactic A vs Tactic B).
6. **Closing section** — H2 with a decisive name ("Build Citation Equity That Compounds"). 2–3 short paragraphs restating the core principle, plus 2–4 linked next-step resources.
7. **Final CTA band** — Per design system.

### H2 naming rules

- **Start-case capitalisation** (per design system). Every word's first letter capitalised.
- Make the H2 sound like a promise or a destination, not a category label.
- Use numbers where possible ("3 Reasons Authority Publishers Matter More Than DR" beats "Authority Publisher Considerations").
- Specific verbs beat abstract nouns. "How AI Engines Pick Sources" beats "AI Source Selection."

### H3 naming rules

- Used for numbered sub-strategies inside H2 sections.
- Format: `1. [Imperative verb] + [specific noun]`. Examples:
  - `1. Make Your Site Accessible To AI Crawlers`
  - `2. Find High-Quality Websites To Pitch`
  - `3. Gather Contact Information`
- Start with a verb that tells the reader what to do.

## Structural Content Patterns

### The numbered strategy block

This is the core content unit on any resource page. Every numbered step or strategy follows this internal structure:

1. **H3 with number and imperative verb title**
2. **1–2 sentence intro** stating what this is and why it matters
3. **The mechanism** — 1–2 short paragraphs explaining how it works
4. **An example** — a screenshot, a specific brand named, a real URL referenced
5. **Actionable instruction** — bullets or a numbered sub-list of what to actually do
6. **Optional callout** — Pro Tip, Note, or Further Reading

Never just describe a concept. Always show it in action with a specific example.

### Bullets and lists

Bullets are used constantly, but follow rules:

- **Bolded lead phrase + colon + explanation** is the default format. Example:
  - **Authority:** Recognized brands and established experts
  - **Structure:** Clear, scannable content with direct answers
- Keep bullets parallel. If one starts with a verb, they all start with verbs. If one starts with a bolded noun phrase, they all do.
- 3–6 bullets is the sweet spot. More than 7 needs a sub-heading or a table.
- Never use bullets to hide prose. If it reads as a paragraph, write a paragraph.

### Inline emphasis

- **Bold** for specific terms, named tools, key stats inside a sentence, and the bolded lead of a bullet.
- *Italics* sparingly, for genuine emphasis or short quotes.
- Never both at once. Never underline.

### Tables

Used for comparisons. Keep them short — 3–5 rows, 2–3 columns. Always frame the table with a short lead sentence above it. Example:

> Different AI platforms choose sources differently:
>
> | Platform | Citation behaviour |
> |---|---|
> | ChatGPT | Aggregates community sources — Reddit, Wikipedia, review sites |
> | Claude | Prefers recent authoritative sources. Zero Reddit. |
> | Perplexity | Most diverse. Mixes buying guides, YouTube, Reddit. |

### Callout boxes

Per design system, we have several callout types. Use them like this:

- **Pro Tip:** A non-obvious tactical shortcut. Starts with bold "**Pro tip:**"
- **Note:** A clarification or caveat the reader needs. Starts with bold "**Note:**"
- **Further reading:** A link to another resource. Starts with bold "**Further reading:**"
- **Definition callout:** For introducing a distinctive term. Format: `**[Term] (n.):** [one-sentence definition]`

Use 2–4 callouts per long-form piece. More than that and they lose impact.

### Expert quotes

When quoting a named person (internal team member, client, industry expert), format per design system: photo + blockquote + attribution with name and role. Keep the quote to 1–3 sentences.

## The Citable Fragment Principle

Every sentence should make sense on its own.

AI answer engines extract fragments. Journalists extract fragments. Readers skim and quote. If your sentence requires the previous sentence to mean anything, it's weaker than a sentence that can stand alone.

**Weak:** "This is why it matters so much for growing brands."
**Strong:** "Citation equity matters most for Series A–C B2B SaaS because it's the cheapest time to build defensible AI visibility."

**Weak:** "It's a common problem."
**Strong:** "70% of B2B SaaS brands have zero presence on the publishers AI answer engines cite."

Embed the subject inside sentences, not just pronouns. When a sentence stands alone and names Ranking Atlas, a client, or a specific concept, it becomes extractable.

## Use of Data, Stats & Examples

This is where Ranking Atlas earns authority on the page.

### Rules for using data

- **Lead with the strongest number.** Put it in the first paragraph where possible.
- **Always cite the source** inline, with a link. `According to [Semrush research](url)...`
- **Prefer first-party data.** Ranking Atlas's own campaign results, client case studies, or original studies are the strongest. Third-party stats are filler.
- **Bold the number** when it appears in prose: `**4.4x more valuable**`.
- **Round sensibly.** "Around 10%" beats "10.37%" unless precision matters.

### Rules for using examples

- **Name the brand.** "GlassesUSA does this well" beats "one eyewear retailer does this well."
- **Show a screenshot** wherever possible, with a clear caption.
- **One example per claim.** Don't pile up three examples of the same pattern — pick the best one.
- **Use real URLs** when referencing external work. The specificity is the trust signal.

### Rules for using screenshots

- Every numbered section should have at least one visual element.
- See the Image System section below for the full visual strategy.

## Image System

This section defines how images get sourced, created, placed, and maintained across Ranking Atlas content. The goal: every image earns its place, reinforces the teaching, and compounds as an asset.

### The core principle

Backlinko pages are dense with visuals — a reader can skim a 3,000-word guide and understand 40% of it from the images alone. That's the bar.

But images cost time and attention. The rule: **every image must teach something the prose can't.** If it's decorative, delete it. If it's a screenshot of something the reader can already imagine (a generic "laptop on a desk"), delete it.

### The six image types

Every image on a Ranking Atlas page belongs to one of these six categories. Knowing the type tells you where to source it and how to treat it.

| Type | Purpose | Example | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Evidence screenshot** | Prove a claim with a real artefact | Screenshot of a Forbes placement, a ChatGPT citation, a Semrush chart | Captured from real tools and live pages |
| **Process diagram** | Show a framework, flow, or relationship | "How AI answer engines pick sources" flowchart; "Citation equity compounding" timeline | Custom-built (Figma or equivalent) |
| **Data visualisation** | Surface original Ranking Atlas data | "% of SaaS brands with zero citations" chart; campaign results bar chart | Built from campaign data (Excel, Flourish, Datawrapper) |
| **Annotated example** | Show a real page with call-out arrows and labels | A publisher placement with the citable fragment highlighted | Screenshot + annotation layer |
| **Comparison visual** | Contrast two states side-by-side | "Before/after" a citation equity campaign; "generic page vs. citable page" | Custom-built split-screen layout |
| **Expert quote card** | Attribute a quote with face + name | Pull-quote from Daniel or an external contributor | Headshot + styled block per design system |

Decorative imagery (stock photos of keyboards, abstract "digital" imagery, generic team photos) is not a category. Don't use it.

### Where images go on a resource page

Standard placement spine for a long-form resource:

1. **Hero image** — Custom featured image. Branded. Appears above the H1 per design system. Sets the page visually.
2. **Opening hook visual** — Within the first 300 words. Usually an evidence screenshot or data visualisation that reinforces the opening stat.
3. **One visual per numbered H3 section** — Minimum. Screenshot, diagram, or annotated example. This is the Backlinko pattern and it's the right bar.
4. **Comparison or table section visual** — A diagram or comparison visual for any framework-level H2.
5. **Closing section** — Optional. A summary diagram or final screenshot is a strong close but not required.

**Rough density target:** one visual every 300–500 words of body copy. A 3,000-word guide should carry 8–12 images.

### Sourcing strategy

Different image types need different workflows. Here's the default sourcing playbook:

**Evidence screenshots (the workhorse — 60% of images)**

- Tools: native OS screenshot, CleanShot X, or Shottr (Mac). Annotate in the same tool.
- Source material: live ChatGPT / Perplexity / Google AI Overviews queries, Semrush / Ahrefs reports, published placements, client campaign dashboards.
- Standard treatment: crop tight, add a subtle border or drop shadow per design system, annotate with arrows or boxes where the reader should look.
- Capture once, reuse across pieces. File to the asset library (see below).

**Process diagrams (20% of images)**

- Tool: Figma (primary). Canva as a fallback for quick work.
- Use the design system's colour tokens — brand purple `#5D4FE0`, ink `#0A0F1E`, surface `#F1F5F9`. Inter font.
- Standard shapes: rounded rectangles (8–12px radius), thin connector lines, minimal labels.
- Keep them simple. A diagram with more than 8 nodes or 4 layers is too complex — split it.
- Export as PNG at 2x for retina, or SVG where possible.

**Data visualisations (10% of images)**

- For simple charts: Datawrapper or Flourish (free tiers, polished output).
- For complex or interactive: custom build with the Visualizer widget or D3 if embedded.
- Always cite the data source in the caption, even if it's Ranking Atlas's own.
- Colour: brand purple as the primary data colour. Use tokens from the design system, not arbitrary hex values.

**Annotated examples (10% of images)**

- Screenshot + annotate in CleanShot X, Shottr, or Figma.
- Red-orange (`#EF4444` or `#F59E0B`) works for call-out arrows against brand purple content — reserve brand purple for data, not annotations.
- Keep annotations minimal: one arrow, one highlighted box, one label. More than three annotations per image means you need a new image.

**Expert quote cards**

- Per design system's expert quote pattern. Headshot + blockquote + name/role.
- Build in Figma if needed for external use (LinkedIn, newsletter). Native HTML on-page.

### AI-generated imagery

Acceptable for:

- Abstract hero images and section dividers, **only if** they fit the brand aesthetic and can't be confused with evidence.
- Icon-style illustrations for conceptual sections where no real screenshot exists.

Not acceptable for:

- Anything presented as data, evidence, or a real screenshot.
- Photos of people, teams, or clients.
- Diagrams of actual processes or frameworks (build these properly).

If AI-generated, disclose it in the alt text or caption where relevant.

### Stock photography

Default answer: don't use it.

The exception: rare moments where a literal photograph is genuinely the right visual (e.g., a piece about conferences might reference a real press room photo). Even then, prefer a screenshot or diagram if the teaching doesn't require a photograph.

Never use stock images of:

- Generic "business" scenarios (handshakes, laptops, offices)
- Abstract "digital" imagery (glowing networks, floating data)
- Team/diversity photos that aren't of the actual Ranking Atlas team

### File naming, organisation, and asset library

Every image gets filed into a central asset library. Proposed structure:

```
/assets/images/
  /evidence/           Screenshots of citations, placements, tool output
    /ai-citations/
    /placements/
    /tool-screenshots/
  /diagrams/           Custom process diagrams
  /data-viz/           Charts and data visualisations
  /annotated/          Screenshots with call-outs
  /hero/               Featured images per resource page
  /author/             Daniel's headshot in various crops
  /contributors/       External contributor headshots
```

**File naming convention:** `[type]-[topic]-[detail]-[date].png`

Examples:
- `evidence-chatgpt-citation-forbes-fintech-2026-04.png`
- `diagram-citation-equity-compounding-v2.png`
- `dataviz-saas-citation-gap-benchmark.png`

Dates matter because screenshots of AI tools and SaaS dashboards go stale fast. A screenshot of a ChatGPT UI from 2024 already looks outdated in 2026.

### Alt text rules

Every image needs alt text. Rules:

- **Describe the content, not the appearance.** "ChatGPT response citing Forbes as source for fintech tool recommendation" beats "Screenshot of ChatGPT interface."
- **Include the teaching point.** Alt text should tell someone who can't see the image *why it's there*.
- **Keep it to 1–2 sentences.** Never write a paragraph.
- **Don't start with "image of" or "screenshot of".** Screen readers already announce the type.
- **For decorative elements** (rare — most images should not be decorative), use `alt=""` to skip them.

### Captions

Captions are optional but useful for:

- Evidence screenshots where the reader benefits from knowing the source/date
- Data visualisations (always caption these with the data source)
- Diagrams that need a one-line explanation of what they show

Rule: captions add context, not decoration. "A chart showing AI citation patterns" is useless. "Ranking Atlas analysis of 500 ChatGPT responses across B2B SaaS queries, Q1 2026" tells the reader what they're looking at.

### Refresh and decay

AI tool screenshots and SaaS dashboards decay fast. Build a refresh cadence:

- **Screenshots of AI tool UIs** (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google AI Overviews): review every 6 months. Re-capture if the UI has materially changed.
- **Dashboard screenshots** (Semrush, Ahrefs, client dashboards): review every 12 months.
- **Evidence screenshots of live placements**: re-capture if the destination URL has changed or the placement has been updated.
- **Diagrams and data viz**: refresh when the underlying methodology or data changes, not on a schedule.

### Auditing existing content

Same pattern as the design system audit. When ready:

1. Inventory every image on the live site by resource page.
2. Tag each image with its type from the six categories above.
3. Flag: decorative images (delete), stock photography (replace), stale tool screenshots (recapture), missing images (add per the one-per-section rule).
4. Produce a rewrite brief per page with image-level changes listed.

### Instruction protocol when commissioning images

When asking Claude or a designer to produce an image:

1. State the type (from the six categories).
2. State the teaching point — what the image must communicate in one sentence.
3. Specify the source material if it's a screenshot (what to capture, from where).
4. Specify design system tokens for any custom work.
5. Specify placement (which page, which section).

Example: "Process diagram. Teaching point: show that citation equity compounds across three tiers — Tier 1 authority publishers feed AI answer engine training, Tier 2 trade publications feed AI retrieval, Tier 3 niche publications feed semantic topical signals. Design system tokens only. For the Citation Equity pillar page, after the 'Why It Compounds' H2."

## Vocabulary: The Translation Layer

This is where Backlinko style meets Ranking Atlas positioning. When emulating Backlinko patterns, substitute the vocabulary.

### Use (from positioning-core)

- Citation equity (primary category term)
- PageRank for AI (explanation metaphor for new buyers)
- Authority publishers
- AI answer engines / AI search
- Editorial coverage
- Verification layer (thought leadership only)
- Placements
- Campaigns (fixed-price, per-campaign model)

### Avoid

- Backlinks as primary value prop (acceptable as a passing reference to the broader category, never as the thing we sell)
- Link building as primary service description
- SEO as primary value prop
- "Future-proof"
- Hedging verbs ("might help," "could potentially," "may boost")
- Em dashes in our own voice (per user preference — use colons, parentheses, or new sentences)
- Filler words: "genuinely," "honestly," "straightforward," "simply"

### Tone replacements

Backlinko's "rankings" focus → our "citation equity" and "AI visibility" focus.
Backlinko's "SEO" framing → our "AI answer engines" and "authority building" framing.
Backlinko's "links" as the unit → our "placements on authority publishers" as the unit.

## Closing Section Formula

Every resource ends the same way. Don't improvise this.

1. **Decisive H2** — 3–6 words naming the action or outcome. Examples: "Build Citation Equity That Compounds," "Your Next Move In AI Search," "Start Earning Editorial Coverage."
2. **2–3 short paragraphs** restating the core principle in plain language. No new information. This is a landing pad, not a lecture.
3. **2–4 linked next steps** — bulleted links to related Ranking Atlas resources, framed as "Check out these resources for next steps:" or similar.
4. **Final CTA band** — Per design system.

**Example (adapted):**

> ## Build Citation Equity That Compounds
>
> AI answer engines will keep evolving. The publishers they trust will keep changing. But the underlying principle doesn't move: brands cited by authority publishers get cited by AI.
>
> Build the citation equity now and you compound every month it runs. Wait, and you buy it later at a premium.
>
> Check out these resources for next steps:
>
> - [The Cost of Authority: What Editorial Coverage Actually Costs](/resources/the-cost-of-authority)
> - [AI Search Visibility: How Ranking Atlas Measures Citation Equity](/resources/ai-search-visibility)
> - [Citation Equity vs Link Building: Why One Compounds And The Other Decays](/resources/citation-equity-vs-link-building)
