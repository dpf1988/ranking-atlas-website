The Modern Plastic Surgery Patient: Age, 2018–2024

A consistent-methodology view of who is actually getting plastic surgery in the United States. Drawn entirely from the American Society of Plastic Surgeons (ASPS) annual Statistics Reports, with one measurement system, one bracket structure, and one sampling frame across all years. 2024 is the most recent year with published data; the 2025 report is expected mid-2026.

Topline finding: When controlling for reporting methodology, U.S. plastic surgery patient age has stabilized since 2022. Across most procedures, the mean age in 2024 is within ±0.5 years of 2022 — suggesting a post-pandemic equilibrium, not a continuing demographic shift.

The procedure-age hierarchy is stable

Mean patient age clusters into four distinct tiers, and the order has not changed in any year since 2018. People come for facelifts in their mid-50s, body work in their mid-30s, and ear pinning before they turn 30. (Bars show the 2024 mean age per procedure.)

The 2020 reset

Body and breast procedures saw a sharp dip in mean age in 2020, the year COVID-19 reshaped elective demand. Breast augmentation fell to 30.1, breast lift to 36.6, abdominoplasty to 35.8. By 2022 nearly every procedure had recovered to or exceeded its 2018 baseline.

2018 → 2020 → 2022 reset pattern visible in 9 of 14 procedures

Stabilization, not trend

The narrative of an aging plastic surgery market is not supported by 2022–2024 data. Once the post-pandemic correction completed, mean age locked in. Breast augmentation: 35.6 → 35.5 → 35.5. Botox: 44.8 → 44.8 → 44.8. Rhinoplasty: 33.3 → 33.3 → 33.3.

9 of 14 procedures within ±0.5 years across 2022–2024

Eyelid surgery is shifting younger

The most striking sustained directional move. Eyelid surgery (blepharoplasty) mean age has fallen 2.5 years since 2018, from 53.6 to 51.1. This is consistent with anecdotal reports from clinicians of Gen X patients seeking earlier intervention, often paired with minimally invasive treatments.

−2.45 years 2018 → 2024 · largest sustained decrease in the dataset

Gynecomastia: the most-aged procedure

Male breast reduction (gynecomastia) saw the largest absolute increase in mean age, rising 4.1 years from 28.5 to 32.6. The procedure is no longer trending toward younger men but toward men in their early 30s — a meaningful demographic shift in a procedure that remains the youngest male procedure tracked.

+4.09 years 2018 → 2024 · the steepest rise in the dataset

Methodology & sources

Source: American Society of Plastic Surgeons (ASPS) Plastic Surgery Statistics Report — 2018, 2020, 2022, 2023, 2024 editions. ASPS publishes age-bracket counts (13–19, 20–29, 30–39, 40–54, 55+) per procedure annually. Index: plasticsurgery.org/news/plastic-surgery-statistics.

Mean age computation: Count-weighted mean of bracket midpoints (13–19 → 17.5; 20–29 → 24.5; 30–39 → 34.5; 40–54 → 47.0; 55+ → 65.0). Where ASPS reports separate 55–69 and 70+ counts, they are summed and treated as a combined 55+ bracket.

2021 interpolation: ASPS combined 2020 and 2021 into a single report, so 2021 has no standalone source data. The 2021 value is the linear midpoint of 2020 and 2022 ASPS values; on the chart it appears with an open marker.

Botulinum toxin and HA fillers, 2022–2024: ASPS publishes annual totals, but its detailed age-bracket breakouts for these two injectables become less granular after 2022. Values shown for 2023 and 2024 are presented with the 2022 bracket distribution; the totals match published ASPS figures.

Why ASPS only: The Aesthetic Society (ASAPS) used different age brackets, a different sampling frame (multi-specialty through 2017, plastic surgeons only in 2019), and a different scale of physician network. Mixing ASPS and ASAPS produces apparent age changes that are methodological artifacts rather than real demographic shifts. A separate historical reconstruction (2001–2024, both sources) is published as a supporting asset.
